# Brand C — Brand Voice & Tone Guide

> *Defense & Government (name TBD).* Derives from `brands/brand-c.md` / the brief.
> Part of the silent **Elyoun Group**. **Gated/forward-looking blueprint.**
> Sober, mission-led — deliberately unlike the games studio. Voice stays constant; tone flexes by channel.

---

## 01 — VOICE OVERVIEW

Brand C sounds like a trusted defense engineer: calm, precise, compliance-aware, and completely without flash. It speaks to people who buy the safest option, not the loudest one, so its authority comes from rigor and proof — never presentation. It is explicit about requirements, capabilities, and limits, and it never reaches for consumer or games language. Where the group's games studio is vivid and energetic, Brand C is sober and exact, because in this segment over-claiming is the fastest way to lose all credibility.

**Voice Essence:** *Brand C sounds like a trusted defense engineer — calm, precise, compliance-aware, and without flash.*

---

## 02 — TONE DIMENSIONS

**Formality** — Casual ←————————●→ Formal
- High. Fuller forms acceptable in formal and contract contexts.
- *Example:* "The system is accredited to [standard] and meets the program's stated training requirements."

**Energy** — Calm ←●————————→ Energetic
- Calm throughout. No urgency, no excitement — steadiness signals reliability.
- *Example:* "Here is what the system does, how it is secured, and where its limits are."

**Expertise** — Accessible ←—————●——→ Authoritative
- Authoritative and compliance-fluent; speaks the language of requirements and accreditation correctly.
- *Example:* "Data is handled in accordance with [control]; the architecture supports [accreditation] from the first commit."

**Warmth** — Distant ←———●———→ Intimate
- Professional and dependable — a reliable partner, not a personality.

**Humor** — Serious ←●————————→ Witty
- Effectively none in brand/sales. The stakes are too high.

---

## 03 — VOICE QUALITIES

**Mission-led** — Frames everything in the readiness outcome the program is measured on.
- **Do:** "Built for the readiness outcome your program is measured on."
- **Don't:** "A game-changing platform for the warfighter."

**Security-aware** — Speaks to compliance, accreditation, and data handling plainly and correctly.
- **Do:** "Architected for [accreditation] from day one."
- **Don't:** Hand-wave "enterprise-grade security."

**Precise** — Exact about requirements, capabilities, and limits.
- **Do:** "Meets requirements X and Y; Z is out of scope for this phase."
- **Don't:** Imply capability that isn't demonstrable.

**Trustworthy** — Explicit about trade-offs, risks, and timelines; honesty is the trust signal.
- **Do:** "This approach is more secure but extends the timeline. Here's the trade."
- **Don't:** Promise everything and hide the caveats.

**Restrained** — No consumer/games tone, no hype, no spectacle for its own sake.
- **Do:** Let rigor and proof carry it.
- **Don't:** Borrow the games studio's energy.

## 04 — VOCABULARY

**Words we use:** mission · readiness · simulation · training · secure · compliance · accreditation · requirements · reliability · fidelity · classified / controlled (as appropriate) · program · trusted · production

**Words we avoid:** game-changing · revolutionary · "metaverse" · "cool" · any consumer-games framing · unprovable capability or security claims · hype superlatives

**Brand-specific language:** Speak to the **program** and the **requirement**. Distinguish a **demonstration** (in a compliant setting) from a **deployed system**. Reference **accreditation/clearance** plainly and only when held. Never use the games studio's "worlds/play" register.

---

## 05 — WRITING STYLE RULES

- **Sentence form:** Formal, precise, complete sentences. Trade-offs and limits stated explicitly.
- **Punctuation:** No exclamation marks.
- **Numbers:** Numerals for measurable claims and requirements.
- **Voice:** Active (passive only where the actor is genuinely irrelevant).
- **Format:** Light-mode, print-legible documents for procurement — built to be read on paper and filed.
- **Claims:** Only what is demonstrably true and currently held.

## 07 — BEFORE AND AFTER

**Before:** "Our game-changing XR platform is a paradigm shift for defense."
**After:** "Immersive simulation built to [accreditation] standards — realism your people can train on, security your program can certify."
*Why:* Replaces hype with the dual, certifiable promise.

**Before:** "Best-in-class, next-gen training for the warfighter!"
**After:** "Training built for the readiness outcome your program is measured on."
*Why:* Mission-led and specific, not a boast.

**Before:** "Military-grade security you can trust."
**After:** "Architected for [accreditation] from the first commit; data handled per [control]."
*Why:* Names the actual standard instead of an empty phrase.

**Before:** "We can do anything your program needs."
**After:** "Meets requirements X and Y today; Z is scoped for a later phase."
*Why:* Precise about scope and limits — the trust signal in procurement.

---

## 08 — THINGS WE NEVER SAY

- **Consumer/games framing** — "worlds," "play," "next-level," "cool," "metaverse."
- **Hype superlatives** — "game-changing," "revolutionary," "best-in-class."
- **Unprovable capability or security claims** — anything not demonstrably true and currently held.
- **Clearance/accreditation claims that aren't real** — the single fastest way to lose credibility in this segment.
- **Exclamation-mark excitement** or manufactured urgency.
- **Over-promising scope** — limits and trade-offs are stated, not hidden.

> **Activation gate:** nothing about defense capability, clearances, or compliance is published until it is demonstrably true (see `BRAND_MESSAGING.md` §07 and `BRAND_STRATEGY.md` §05/§11).
